Marlies Hartmann who piled on the pounds undergoes dramatic transformation

With her blonde hair, washboard abs and bombshell bikini body it's hard to believe that Marlies Hartmann was once obese.

More than 12 years ago the California-based single mom, who now runs a health food-focused website, was the size of a heavyweight boxer, tipping the scales at 218lbs.

But incredibly the 32-year-old, who lives in Los Angeles, managed to lose 75lbs – and keep it off – by ditching low-fat diet foods.

Then and now: Marlies Hartmann admits that she was first put on a diet at age eight, however her weight continued to balloon as a teen (L). She has since shed more than 75lbs however (R)

Now she's motivating more than 12,500 Instagram followers to do the same by posting photos of her mouth watering dishes and homemade recipes for fudge brownies, decadent smoothies and ice cream sandwiches.

But far from being unhealthy or full of fat, almost all Marlies' recipes are vegan and gluten free.

It's a far cry from the blogger's early life, which she says was marred by yo-yo dieting, binge-eating and failed weight loss attempts.

'I was put on my first diet when I was eight,' she tells Daily Mail Online. 'I went to the doctor and they handed me this sheet of foods that diabetics should eat.

'It was like canned tuna and cottage cheese and a list of fruits and vegetables.'

Marlies, who is not diabetic, adds: 'None of this meant anything except I knew there was something wrong with me.

'That was the first time I realized I needed to be dieting.'

The daughter of a German mom and an Italian-American dad, Marlies says 'food was love' in her household.

'My dad would make homemade sauce, Italian sausage, homemade pasta and meatballs,' she explains.

Strict control: When Marlies, pictured as a teen, first visited the doctor, she was given the same kind of diet plan used to help diabetics

Healthy and happy: The mother-of-one is now following a much easier, more enjoyable diet plan, and spends much of her time whipping up delicious, yet healthy, recipes for her blog readers to try

'He would make these big stews and you'd see an inch of oil at the top from either the meat or he would dump in olive oil.

'It was always, "Oh you like it? Have more!"'

Marlies admits that she needed no encouragement, saying: 'To me you would eat until you were stuffed.'

Lulled into the false sense of security that she wouldn't be breaking her diet as long as she ate low-fat foods, Marlies gorged on diet treats.

'The whole low-fat, diet food industry came in the '90s,' she says.

'Suddenly I said, "Well, I can eat a [whole] box of SnackWell's because they're fat-free."'

Her eating really got out of hand, however, in 1992, when she was 10.

That was the year her older sister Michelle died in a motorcycle accident at the age of 22. Shortly afterwards her family moved from Illinois to Utah.

Finding it hard to cope, Marlies turned to food for comfort and by the age of 13 her weight had escalated.

Emotional eating: When Marlies' sister died in 1992, the health blogger admits that she turned to food for comfort and her weight quickly increased. She is pictured right at age 12, and left at age 13

Moving on: In 2002, Marlies read a magazine article which contained a 1,200-calorie diet plan. She immediately decided to give it a go and by 2003 (pictured), she had started to shed an impressive amount of weight

'I ate,' she says. 'It was not knowing or having friends, not knowing where I was, being in such a Mormon environment and feeling like I didn't fit in on top of reaching puberty…

'I probably gained 50lbs that year.

'If you look at a picture of me in sixth grade in the beginning of the year and at the end of the year you see a very big difference in how I looked…

'That was when I went from being chubby to obese.'

Desperate to solve her problem, doctors put her on the diet drug of choice at the time – Fen-Phen, which was later pulled off the market because it damages the heart valves. 

'I was 13 when they put me on Fen-Phen,' Marlies explains.

'I remember in eighth grade being in school and my leg just jostling up and down, having this crazy energy.

'But I wasn't hungry. I lost 30lbs that year and I felt good.'

It wasn't sustainable though and once Fen-Phen was removed from the market, Marlies started gorging again.

For the next few years her weight continued to yo-yo, as she tried everything from the low-carb Atkins diet to bingeing and purging in fruitless efforts to keep her weight under control.

By the time the September 11 terrorist attacks occurred in 2001, Marlies was an art history and theatre student at New York University.

Onward and upward: By the time Marlies was finishing up her time in Florence in 2003 (L), she had dropped to 190lbs, and her weight loss continued for many months to come

Confidence: Marlies admits that she was worried her 2014 divorce and custody battle would de-rail the amazingly healthy path she had started to follow, however it actually helped to give her new motivation

Scared by the events going on around her, she comfort ate.

'At that point it was like the world might end tomorrow so you might as well eat good,' she says.

'That December after September 11 the highest number I remember seeing on the scale was 218lbs, but I probably hit a little more.'

It was in 2002 after reading a Shape article about calories that Marlies started following a 1,200-calorie plan featured in the magazine.

She ditched her mocha lattes, Caesar salad wraps and nachos swimming in cheese, sour cream and guacamole for Slim Fast, soup and salad.

By the time she went to Florence, Italy to spend five months studying art history she was down to the 190s range.

While she was away Marlies finally learned the key to permanent weight loss.

'When I first got there I ate pizza and pasta and olive oil, and pasta and olive oil on my pizza,' she says.

'It was amazing. But that was when I said, 'If I keep eating this way my clothes aren't going to fit'.'

Motivated by her roommate who had recently lost 50lbs on Weight Watchers, Marlies looked for ways to incorporate healthy eating into her lifestyle.

At first they decided to 'BBB – ban the breadbasket' when they ate at restaurants.

Doting parent: The food blogger throws her energy into cooking and creating recipes whenever her daughter is spending time with her father. She sees it as a way of distracting her from any sadness 

Family ties: Marlies no longer feels as though she will fall into the trap of comfort eating when she is sad or stressed

But it was observing how the locals ate that really spurred them on.

'My roommate and I realized that if we stopped eating like tourists and we started eating like the people that live there eat, we're going to lose weight.

'You don't see people who live there just eating pasta and pizza. And if you get pasta it's like this little plate and it's amazing.

Marlies says she started eating 'real food,' shopping in the local market where she'd fill up on fruits and vegetables.

'I really started slowing down', she says. 'There wasn't a TV and there were a lot of nights when we didn't eat out.

'I would set the table, light a candle and cook myself pasta.

'I made this homemade mushroom cream sauce – real food. Not this diet bulls**t.

'And I would sit down and eat it and I would just love it.

'It was the first time I was loving on my food – savoring it, enjoying it and not eating it like, "I can't eat this again so I've just got to shovel it in, pretend like it doesn't happen and then tomorrow I'm going to start my diet."

'That was the shift.'

Marlies started working out – running and walking – to ease the frustration of taking challenging Italian classes.

Fit and active: Marlies started working out when she was living in Florence to ease the frustration of taking challenging Italian classes and continues to indulge in various physical pursuits today 

By the time she came home to the US her clothes didn't fit because they were too loose. In five months she'd gone from a size 16 to a size 12 and weighed in the 170s range.

'I remember going to Old Navy and getting a pair of shorts that were a 12 and that to me was one of the greatest moments ever,' Marlies says.

In summer 2003 she joined Weight Watchers and by 2005 she was a healthy 148lbs.

For years Marlies was able to keep the pounds at bay by diligently keeping a food diary and exercising several times a week.

She kept the weight off, even after having her daughter London in 2009.

But when she split up from her husband in January 2014 she was launched headlong into a bitter divorce and custody battle that she was scared would derail all her hard work.

But – rather than comfort eat – she started cooking, whipping up healthy recipes in her spare time.

'There were a lot of days that I would wake up and be super-emotional and crying,' Marlies says.

'There are nights now that my daughter's not sleeping with me. And when things get really bad I can always make something.

'I can always cook. It gives me this creative outlet and it gives me something [to be] passionate about.

'I was really scared that I would start emotional eating again, which I haven't done in so long.

Tasty treats: Since she started her health food-focused Instagram account in September 2014, Marlies has amassed more than 12,500 followers 

Deliciously healthy: The single mother has thrown herself into creating an array of tasty sweets that are almost all vegan and gluten free

'My biggest fear was that my divorce was going to trigger [that] and it really hasn't.'

Last year Marlies started thinking of ways to support herself as a single mom. Wanting to use her weight loss and maintenance journey to inspire people she decided to become a health coach.

'I could go work for somebody for $12-an-hour like at a clothing shop or I could really build something,' she says.

She enrolled at the Institute for the Psychology of Eating and started studying holistic nutrition.

She became a food blogger by accident – taking photos of the dishes that she was making and posting them on Instagram using the name Hartmann Health.

'I said, "Along with the health coaching I'm just going to post some pictures,"' Marlies says.

She uploaded her first photo in September 2014. And now, seven months later she has over 12,500 followers and is working on a recipe book.

Part of the appeal of her Instagram page is the beautifully shot photos, most of which – in the beginning – Marlies took with her iPhone.

Her boyfriend Fabio Texera is a photographer who gave her some tips on how to take the best pictures of the food she would shoot on her back patio or upstairs in her apartment where natural light filters through the skylight.

But for Marlies the essence of her blog is sharing with her followers that you don't have to eat low-fat or low-carb diet food to be healthy.

You can eat homemade soups and salads, yogurt parfait, pancakes, cookies and cheesecakes; have sushi, indulge in pizza and pasta and lose weight.

Carb loading: Marlies wants her followers and readers to know that you don't have to avoid carbs in order to be healthy

Fresh and vibrant: The recipes featured on Marlies' blog all use good quality ingredients

Marlies, who eats a mostly plant-based diet occasionally adding eggs, fish and chicken, says: 'I'm not a trained cook. Everything's experimenting.

'It's not that my things are healthy or low fat or diet in any way. They're real food.

'But to me that makes you more connected. The more you can have real food in your food the more nutrients you're going to get.

'Your body assimilates the nutrients so your body feels less deprived and you don't want to keep eating more and more of it.

'Less makes you feel satisfied. And you're just more connected.

'It's creating this mindful connection to what you're doing and how you're eating that makes you appreciate it more.

'You're not going to shovel down a piece of cheesecake that you soaked cashews for four hours [to make].'

Marlies admits that she hasn't got all the answers but she wants to inspire people to change their attitude towards food just as she did while studying in Florence.

She says: 'I'm not perfect. I ate pizza last night for dinner and [drank] champagne.

'But it's real life and the difference is I'm no longer beating myself up for it.'
